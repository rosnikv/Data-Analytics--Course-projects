      Let me take a shot at explaining the Chi-square test, with a focus on intuition, rather than the formula. I will give a short answer and also a more elaborate one for those who are interested.

      First, There are two types of chi square tests:
      1. thegoodness-of-fit test (does a coin tossed 20 times turn up 10 heads and 10 tails?)
      2. the test of independence (is there a relationship between gender and salary?)
       
      They are both closely related, but for the sake of developing an intuitive understanding, let’s look at the “test of independence” chi-square statistic.
       
      Intuition if you want to  read only one paragraph:
      We have some outcomes, and a variable that we think might have an effect. We look at observed values of the outcome, with and without our variable of interest. We then calculate the expected values. From that we calculate the deviations from what we observed. (There are bound to be some deviations.) We scale the deviations based on expected values, and adjust for number of sets of samples. The chi-square statistic is one measure of that deviation. (Whatever we are observing, is it random or is it unlikely to be random?)
      Statisticians (using probability and combinatorics) have already calculated a look-up table, called the chi-squared table. Given a certain observed deviation, that table tells us what is the probability that this deviation is due to chance. If that probability is really really tiny, then we say “Hey, that can’t be due to chance. There must be some sort of an effect.” If the p-value is big, we say “This could be purely due to chance. No need to get excited about this.” That in a nutshell is how Chi-square works: It is a measure of deviation, compared against pre-computed values that tells us how probable these deviations are.


      Read on, if you want to know more about the thinking behind the steps of Chi-square statistic, (if you need it for school!) The paragraph above is not enough.
       
      Intuition about when to use the Chi-squared test
      We want to tell if Variable A has had an effect on Outcome B. (Or could it be purely due to randomness.)
      Chi-square tests help us answer these types of questions.
       
      Canonical Examples: Did the new drug help reduce some disease metric?
      Does smoking during pregnancy lead to reduced baby-weight?

      To fully grasp a chi-square test, you should understand the ideas behind the three main steps:
      1. Building a "contingency table"
      2. Calculating the chi-square parameter
      3. Looking up a p-value for the chi-square

      Understanding Contingency Tables
      A contingency table is the term for grouping our observations by categories. In a 2x2 table, the rows are “with” and “without” new treatment. The 2 columns could be the presence or absence of improvement to the disease.
      There are two contingency tables.
      One is what we experimentally OBSERVED.
      The other is what we EXPECTED to happen.
       
      Let’s take an easy to understand example. 30 people are given a new drug, and 30 more are not given a drug. Some improve and some don’t.
       
      OBSERVED CONTINGENCY TABLE
       
                                          Improvement | No-Improvement
      With new drug                15                     15
      Without drug                  05                     25
       
      The question is, did this new drug help?
       
      Only chi-square can help us get closer to the answer. What did we EXPECT?
      In our example, of the 20 patients who showed improvement, we should expect  10 and 10 in each row (equally spread) and similarly, we expect the 40 patients to be 20 and 20 in the two cells in the no-improvement column.
       
      CONTINGENCY TABLE OF EXPECTATIONS
                                          Improvement | No-Improvement
      With new drug                10                     20
      Without drug                  10                     20
       
      Now, we can do a cell-by-cell comparison of the two tables. In our simple example, the difference in each cell is 5.
      The Chi-square value works out to be 7.5 (The Sum of (diff^2/expected value))
      The intuition behind dividing by the expected value is to scale it down. Otherwise squares of differences can get quite large, and we won't have a basis for knowing if those differences are small or too large.
       
      Intuition about Chi-square value
       Very large deviations are surprising, unexpected. It is possibly not due to chance alone.
      Small chi-square values (small deviations) are absolutely expected, and so they can be attributed to randomness.
       
      Okay, but how small is small and how big is too big? We need a sense of scale. That is why we need the Chi-square table. In our example, is 7.5 big or small?
       
      Intuition about the Chi-square table
      The table has many many rows, but here's a small portion of it
      p-value 0.995 0.99 0.975 0.95 0.90 0.10 0.05 0.025 0.01 0.005 1
       df = 1  --- --- 0.001 0.004 0.016 2.706 3.841 5.024 6.635 7.879
       
      It is a look-up table. So we come with a Chi-square (deviation) value and read off a “p-value” (the probability that this much deviation is due to chance alone.)
      So, for really tiny deviations (Chi-square) we would expect a very high p-value. (The left side of the chi-sq table)
      But the chi-square better be HUGE if we going to confidently assert that it is due to the variables effect, and NOT due to chance alone. So, for miniscule p-value, we expect gigantic deviations, and we do see that in the table.
       
      In short, the table tells us the probability that what we observed could have happened simply by chance.

      Intuition about Degrees of Freedom
      Very broadly, the degree of freedom is about:
      Did you take enough samples? Did you take it a sufficient number of times?
       
      Df = Number of columns-1. If I give you the total and all values but 1 column, that value is deterministic. Same for Rows. Df is : Number of rows -1
       For the whole contingency table, df is (numrows-1) * (numcols -1)
       
      In our example, df is 1. For 7.5, we can read off a p-value of approx. 0.004
       
      That is quite small. There is only a 0.4% chance that what we observed is purely due to randomness. The new drug does seem to have an effect.
       
      So that is the intuition behind how Chi-square tests work -- by comparing observed deviations against expected deviations.

      The chi-squared test compares a set of frequencies that has been observed with a set of frequencies predicted by theory. The test involves four steps.

      First, we look at the difference between the two sets of frequencies. We don't add them all up yet, however, because the total of the differences is not helpful. For example, if I toss a coin ten times and get four heads and six tails, then the number of heads is low by 1 and the number of tails is high by 1 so the total deviation from what we expect is 0. This will always happen.

      So, second, we square the differences. We still don't add them up yet because we have no way to interpret the total. If we get a total of 10 is that a lot? We need a context for it. If I owe you some money and underpay you by £10 will you be mad? Well, if I owed you £11 then you'd be very mad. If I owed you £1,000,000 you probably wouldn't even notice.

      So, third, we divide the squared differences by what we expected we'd get. This gives them a context. And we're now safe to add them up. But the more categories we have observations for, the higher this total is likely to be because we're adding up more things.

      So, fourth, we compare the total with a value based on the number of categories. If our total is bigger than this value then the deviations from what we expected were very high and this suggests our expectations were wrong. If the total is smaller then it suggests that what we observed was close to what we expected and there's no reason to doubt our expectations.
