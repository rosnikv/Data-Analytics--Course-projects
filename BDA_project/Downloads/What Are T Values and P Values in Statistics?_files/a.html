                                  prunkel alexander appiah • 12 days ago

                                  I'm not sure I understand your question entirely. But yes, if you are using a hypothesis test then you want the results to be statistically significant. If the results are statistically significant, it means that you have enough evidence from your sample data to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the alternative hypothesis (which is usually the hypothesis that you're trying to prove or demonstrate) holds for the entire population that is represented by your sample. Typically, statistical significance is demonstrated by comparing a test statistic such as a T value (or Z value or F value or chi-square value) with a critical value, which is determined before testing.                                           prunkel alexander appiah • 11 days ago

                                          The beta coefficient can mean different things in statistics, depending on the area, the analysis, and the application. So I’m not exactly sure what you’re referring to. But I think the point you’re trying to make is that suppose the strength of an association or the correlation between two variables is strong (that is, the magnitude of the correlation is considerably greater than 0). Doesn’t that also count as evidence for the strength of association, regardless of statistical significance? The short answer is yes. Statistical significance is not everything. Suppose you found an estimated correlation of .95 between two variables. That’s a strong positive correlation value (very close to 1). But suppose the p-value was not small enough for the results to be statistically significant. That does not mean that you conclude there is no correlation. It means only that *you do not have enough evidence* to reject the null hypothesis that the correlation is 0 and no linear relationship exists. You do not have enough evidence to conclude that the results are statistically significant—however the correlation itself appears to be very strong. But maybe you need a larger sample or reduced variability in the data to attain statistical significance for that strong association. Conversely, suppose you find a very weak correlation such as 0.002 between two variables but the results are statistically significant (perhaps because you have a very large sample). Even though the results are statistically significant, the strength of the association is very weak, very close to 0, and may be practically trivial.
